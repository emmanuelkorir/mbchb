### Question 1

A 5 year old girl presents to you in the outpatient clinic with mild fevers on and off; occasional pain on passing urine. persistenly foul smelling urine. The last 2 days mother reports that child has been unable to control the urge to pass urine and has been making frequent visits to the bathroom. This is not the first time this child has been managed for similar symptoms.

a. What is the most likely diagnosis? Give 3 features from the history above to support your answer. (4 marks)

<details>
  <summary>Check Answer</summary>
  UTI
</details>

b. Give 8 risk factors for above condition (4 Marks)

<details>
  <summary>Check Answer</summary>
  - Previous UTI: history suggestive of, or a confirmed UTI
  - Recurrent fever of unknown origin
  - Immunosuppression: diabetes, HIV
  - Constipation causing urinary obstruction 
  - Spinal lesions 
  - Male (uncircumcised, less than a year)
  - Vesicoureteral reflux
  - Hygiene? 
</details>

c. Explain how you would take a urine sample in this child (3 marks)

<details>
  <summary>Check Answer</summary>
  - Suprapubic
  - In and out catheter
  - Midstream urine 
</details>

d. What investigations would you carry out in this child and which test would give you a definitive diagnosis (6 Marks)?

<details>
  <summary>Check Answer</summary>
  - Basic bloodwork: FHG, differential WBC. 
  - CRP, ESR.
  - Urine macroscopy, dipstick and urinalysis
  - Urine microscopy, culture and sensitivity: positive urine culture is the gold standard. 
  Imaging:
  - Ultrasound: if normal, no further test 
  - Ultrasound: if abnormal and dilatation seen, MCUG. Other abnormality, refer for DMSA.
 - If ultrasound normal and patient comes back with UTI, repeat ultrasound and DMSA should be done 
</details>
