2. A five year old girl who has recently started school presents in the emergency room with a four days history of fever, cough, conjunctivitis and skin rash appearing first on the face and spreads to the trunk. The child is not in respiratory failure. The school had reported some other children having the same problem.

i. What is the most likely diagnosis? (2 marks)

<details>
<summary>Check Answer</summary>

Measles

</details>

ii. Outline how you would manage her. (10 marks)

<details>
<summary>Check Answer</summary>

Supportive

Admit, Isolate to prevent airbone transmisssion

ABC(circulation-IV fluids)

Treat secondary infections(otitis media, bacterial pneumonia)with antibiotics

Pain management-analgesics

Hyperthermia-exposure, antipyretics

If malnourished: WHO 10 steps, commercially prepared F75, F100, RUTF

Definitive

Vitamin A supplementation 

Antiviral therapy - ribavirin(use should be considered experimental)

Post-exposure prophylaxis for unvaccinated contacts: human immunoglobulin, measles vaccine


</details>

iii. What organ systems and how are they likely to be affected by complications of this condition (10 marks)

<details>
<summary>Check Answer</summary>

CNS - Acute encephalitis, subacute sclerosing panencephalitis, meningitis

Respiratory - Pneumonia, bronchitis, laryngotracheobronchitis(croup), exacerbation of tb

GIT - Diarrhoea, hepatitis, acute pancreatitis, appendicitis

Eyes - Keratitis, corneal ulceration, optic neuritis, retinitis, blindness

Skin - Secondary bacterial infections, postinflammatory pigmentary changes, scarring

ENT - Otitis media, otitis externa, mastoiditis

CVS - Myocarditis, pericarditis

</details>

iv. State what and how the Kenya government routinely does to prevent this condition (3 marks)

<details>
<summary>Check Answer</summary>

Vaccination against measles rubella administered at 9 months and 18 months or at 6 months in the event of a measles outbreak or HIV exposure

Vitamin A supplementation at 6 months then every 6 months upto 5 Years

Health education on danger signs such as fever , difficulty in breathing

</details>

3. A 4 year old child who has lived in Europe since birth presents to Paediatric casualty 10 days after coming from Kisumu with a temperature of 38.9°c and chills. The child looks pale and has respiratory distress.

i. State the most likely diagnosis? (1 marks)

<details>
<summary>Check Answer</summary>

Malaria

</details>

ii. State 3 priority investigations you would carry out on him and why? (6 marks)

<details>
<summary>Check Answer</summary>

CBC - Hb levels

PBF -  malaria gametocytes

Rapid dignostic tests - histidine rich protein 2(HRP2) from P. falciparumand plasmodium lactate dehydrogenase(pLDH) antigens

</details>

iii. Outline his management plan (12 marks)

<details>
<summary>Check Answer</summary>

Supportive

Admit

ABCD: Breathing -oxygen if Spo2 less than 90%, Circulation- if severe pallor/ Hb less than 5g/dl give 10mls/kg of pRBC or 20mls/kg of whole blood over 4 hours, give maintenance fluids as you await blood. D - if hypoglycemic or blood glucose less than 2.5mmol/l give 5mls/kg of 10% dextrose over 5 minutes

Give antipyretics or expose in case of hyperthermia

Definitive

IV/IM artesunate if less than 20kg give 3mg/kg, if more than 20kg give 2.4mg/kg at 0, 12, 24,hours then daily for a mamximum of seven days. Can be given for a minimum of 24 hours, if able to drink change to artemisinin combination therapy: arthemeter and lumefantrine 20mg/120mg  at 0hr then at 8hr then 12 hourly on day 2 and 3.

Quinine loading dose 20mg/kg over 4 hours then 10mg/kg over 2 hours 8 hourly. 

Second line antimalarials: Dihydroartemisinin and piperaquine

</details>

iv. What are the likely prevention strategies for such visiting children? (6 marks)

<details>
<summary>Check Answer</summary>

Chemoprophylaxis - mefloquine (2-3 weeks before travel and throughout the period of travel and 4 weeks after travel), atovaquone-proguanil (one day before travel throughout travel and 1 week after), doxycycline (one day before departure, daily and 4 weeks after), chloroquine 

Sleeping under treated mosquito nets

Mosquito repellants

Long-sleeved clothings

Vaccination: RTS,S/AS01 vaccine

</details>

4. A baby born at 38 weeks gestation by dates with a birthweight of 2300 grams is noted to have yellow discoloration of the skin up to the legs within 24 hours of birth. Bilirubin levels are reported as: Total bilirubin 250 micromols/litre and direct bilirubin of 20 micromols/litre.

i. What further history will you need on parents/family, antenatal/birth, and post-natal aspects (9 Marks)

<details>
<summary>Check Answer</summary>

Parents/family - rhesus and blood group of the father and mother, family history of chronic illnesses such as thalassemias/spherocytosis/enzymopathies-G6PD deficiency, pyruvate kinase deficiency

Antenatal - maternal blood group and rhesus, HIV, VDRL, and Hepatitis B, OGTT screening 

Birth history - mode of delivery, birthweight, cried immediately after birth, time of breastfeeding initiation 

Postnatal - stool colour, urine colour, feeding history, jaundice progression

</details>

ii. What non-imaging investigations would you like to carry out? (13 Marks)

<details>
<summary>Check Answer</summary>

Complete blood count: Hb levels, WBC and its differential, platelet count

Reticulocyte count

Proinflammatory markers: ESR, CRP, procalcitonin to rule out infections

Peripheral blood films to check RBC morphology

Serum bilirubin levels: total, direct, indirect

Direct coombs test

Blood culture

Liver function test: AST, ALT, GGT

Triple serology: HIV, HBV, HCV

TORCHES screening: Toxoplasmosis, Rubella, CMV, HSV, Syphilis

Urinalysis

Urine culture

UECs to check renal function

Hb electrophoresis to rule out thalassemias

Coagulation screen

</details>
