### Question 2

a. Define prolonged neonatal jaundice. (2 marks)

<details>
  <summary>Check Answer</summary>
  Prolonged neonatal jaundice is the presence of jaundice for longer than{' '}
  <strong>14</strong> days in a term neonate and more than <strong>
    21
  </strong> days in a pre-term.
</details>

b. List the causes of prolonged jaundice. (10 marks)

<details>
  <summary>Check Answer</summary>
  <p>
    Causes can be divided in to conjugated which presents with dark
    urine and pale stool and causes include extrahepatic biliary duct
    disorders like biliary atresia, choledochal cyst and gallstones.
    Bile duct hypoplasia disorders like Allagile. Neonatal hepatitis.
    Infections. Hypothyroidism. Cystic fibrosis.
  </p>
  <p>
    Causes of unconjugated include physiologic jaundice. Breastmilk
    jaundice. Hemolysis like ABO and rhesus incompatibility. Increased
    enterohepatic circulation like in pyloric stenosis or bowel
    obstruction.
  </p>
</details>

c. What investigations would you do in this condition? (10 marks)

<details>
  <summary>Check Answer</summary>
  <p>
    Overall clinical appearance and vital signs. Skin inspected for
    the level of jaundice. Physical examination for signs of causative
    disorders.
  </p>
  <p>
    Lab investigations include: CBC and differential. Reticulocyte
    count. ESR, CRP & Procalcitonin. Serum bilirubin levels. Liver
    function tests. Coagulation screen. TSH. Blood glucose. Full blood
    count. TORCH screen / Hepatitis serology. Alpha1Antitrypsin level
    and phenotype. Thyroid Function Tests. Random Cortisol. Plasma
    amino acids. Serum iron and ferritin. Urine culture. Urine
    metabolic screen.
  </p>
  <p>
    {' '}
    Radiological: abdominal ultrasound, liver biopsy, MRCP, hepatobiliary
    scintigraphy.
  </p>
</details>

d. List the complications of prolonged jaundices (2 marks)

<details>
  <summary>Check Answer</summary>
  <ul>
    <li>Acute bilirubin encephalopathy(ABE)</li>
    <ul>
      <li>
        Initial phase: hypotonia, lethargy, poor suck, weak/absent mor
      </li>
      <li>
        Intermediate phase: stupor, irritability, increased
        tone(retrocolis & opisthotonus), fever
      </li>
      <li>
        Advance phase: hypertonia, apnea, seizures, deep stupor/coma
      </li>
    </ul>
    <li>Chronic bilirubin encephalopathy/kernicterus</li>
    <ul>
      <li>Choreoathetoid cerebral palsy</li>
      <li>Sensorineural hearing loss</li>
      <li>Palsy of vertical gaze</li>
      <li>Dental enamel hypoplasia</li>
    </ul>
    <li>Acute liver failure</li>
  </ul>
</details>

### Question 5

In regards to the emergency triage assessment and treatment in an outpatient department for children aged less than 5 years.

a. Define triage (1 marks)

<details>
  <summary>Check Answer</summary>
  Triage is the process of rapidly screening sick children soon after their
  arrival in the hospital, in order to identify: a. those with <strong>
    emergency signs
  </strong>, who require immediate emergency treatment, b. those with <strong>
    {' '}
    priority signs
  </strong>, who should be given priority in the queue for treatment, and
  c. those with <strong>non-priority signs</strong>, who can wait for treatment.
</details>

b. List the 3 triage categories and state the importance of triage as related to time spent by the patient before being attended in outpatient department. (6 marks)

<details>
  <summary>Check Answer</summary>
  <ul>
    <li>
      Emergency care - immediate action, taken to emergency area,
      clinician/nurse sees NOW as it is life threatening.
    </li>
    <li>
      Priority care - front of the queue, clinical review as soon as
      possible as can soon become fatal.
    </li>
    <li>
      Non urgent - waits in queue to be seen by the clinician since it
      is not life threatening.
    </li>
  </ul>
</details>

    <li>Coma</li>
    <li>Convulsions</li>

</details>
